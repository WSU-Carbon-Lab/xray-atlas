# Measured and derived quantities

NEXAFS measures X-ray absorption versus photon energy, but the literature and
this database express that absorption in several interconvertible forms. This
page defines each quantity, its units, and the relations the catalog uses.
Photon energy $E$ is reported in electron volts throughout.

## Absorption from transmission

For a beam of intensity $I_0$ traversing a film of thickness $z$, the
transmitted intensity follows the Beer-Lambert law

$$
I = I_0\, e^{-\mu(E)\, z},
$$

defining the linear absorption coefficient $\mu(E)$ with dimensions of inverse
length. The catalog's **optical density** channel is the dimensionless
attenuation

$$
\mathrm{OD}(E) = -\ln\!\bigl(I/I_0\bigr) = \mu(E)\, z .
$$

The **mass absorption coefficient** $\mu/\rho$ (cm² g⁻¹) removes the density
dependence and is the natural channel for comparing materials of different
packing; it connects measured spectra to tabulated atomic values away from the
near-edge region.

## The complex index of refraction

In the X-ray regime the index of refraction is written

$$
n(E) = 1 - \delta(E) + i\,\beta(E),
$$

with both $\delta$ and $\beta$ small and positive for most materials. The
imaginary part carries absorption,

$$
\mu(E) = \frac{4\pi\,\beta(E)}{\lambda} = \frac{4\pi\,\beta(E)\,E}{hc},
$$

while $\delta$ governs refraction and phase. The two are not independent;
causality links them through the Kramers-Kronig relations treated on
[the optical constants page](/wiki/nexafs/optical-constants).

## Atomic scattering factors

For a material of atomic number density $n_a$, the optical constants relate to
the complex atomic scattering factor $f = f_1 + i f_2$ through

$$
\delta = \frac{r_e \lambda^2}{2\pi}\, n_a\, f_1,
\qquad
\beta = \frac{r_e \lambda^2}{2\pi}\, n_a\, f_2,
$$

with $r_e$ the classical electron radius. For molecules the catalog computes
$n_a$ from the chemical formula and the declared or assumed density, which is
why formula metadata on a record enables the derived $f_1$ and $f_2$ channels.

## Dielectric response

To first order in the small quantities $\delta$ and $\beta$,

$$
\varepsilon(E) = n^2 \simeq 1 - 2\delta + 2i\beta,
\qquad
\chi(E) = \varepsilon - 1 \simeq -2\delta + 2i\beta,
$$

giving the dielectric function and susceptibility channels used in scattering
analysis (resonant soft X-ray scattering contrast is built from differences in
$\varepsilon$ between phases). For anisotropic, oriented materials these become
tensor quantities; the scalar channels here describe the orientationally
averaged response.

## Persisted versus derived channels

Browse plots expose an eleven-channel rail in three trays (spectroscopy,
imaginary constants, real constants). **Persisted** channels are written at
ingest or recalculation and plotted as stored, raw absorption, OD, mass
absorption, $\beta$, and $\delta$, each with optional uncertainties.
**Derived** channels, $f_1$, $f_2$, and the real and imaginary parts of
$\varepsilon$ and $\chi$, are computed in the browser from stored $\beta$ and
$\delta$ using the relations above and are not written back to the database.
The mapping from uploaded columns to persisted fields is documented in
[uploading spectroscopy](/wiki/atlas/uploading-data).
