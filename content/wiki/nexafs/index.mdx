# Introduction to NEXAFS

Near-edge X-ray absorption fine structure (NEXAFS) probes transitions of
core-level electrons into unoccupied bound and quasi-bound states just above an
element's absorption edge. Because the initial state is a core orbital with a
well-defined binding energy, the technique is element specific, and because the
final states are shaped by local bonding, the fine structure within roughly
30 eV of the edge reports directly on chemistry, electronic structure, and
molecular orientation.

## The absorption process

An incident photon of energy $E = \hbar\omega$ promotes a core electron (for
the carbon K-edge, a $1s$ electron near 284 eV) into an unoccupied state. In
the electric-dipole approximation the cross section for a transition from
initial state $|i\rangle$ to final state $|f\rangle$ follows Fermi's golden
rule,

$$
\sigma(E) \;\propto\; \bigl|\langle f \,|\, \hat{\mathbf{e}}\cdot\mathbf{r}\, |\, i \rangle\bigr|^{2}\,
\rho_f(E),
$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{e}}$ is the X-ray polarization unit vector and $\rho_f$ the
density of final states. Two consequences organize everything that follows.
Dipole selection rules ($\Delta l = \pm 1$) restrict which final states are
reachable from a given core level, and the scalar product
$\hat{\mathbf{e}}\cdot\mathbf{r}$ makes the transition intensity depend on the
angle between the polarization and the transition dipole moment, which is the
basis of orientation analysis.

## Anatomy of a near-edge spectrum

Below and at the edge, sharp resonances correspond to transitions into low-lying
antibonding states, $\pi^{*}$ resonances typically appearing first and most
intensely in unsaturated systems. Near the ionization threshold, weaker Rydberg
states may appear in molecules with accessible diffuse states. Above threshold,
broader $\sigma^{*}$ shape resonances sit on the continuum step produced by
ionization. Linewidths combine the core-hole lifetime, instrumental resolution,
and vibrational structure, so resonances sharpen or blur depending on edge and
beamline.

## What NEXAFS measures in practice

Edge position and near-edge resonance energies track oxidation state and
chemical environment. Resonance intensities measured with defined polarization
geometry encode molecular orientation and order parameters in thin films and
at interfaces. Comparing detection channels (electron versus photon yield)
adds depth sensitivity. These capabilities make NEXAFS a workhorse for
polymers, organic semiconductors, self-assembled monolayers, and soft-matter
systems studied at synchrotron facilities.
